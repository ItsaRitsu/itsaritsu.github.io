Legal standing

Courts are accepting blockchain timestamps as evidence. Here's what that actually means.

The honest picture is more nuanced than "blockchain is admissible" — and the nuance is actually more useful to your clients than the headline.

Mar 2025

Tribunal de Marseille, France

The first European court to explicitly recognize blockchain timestamping as legitimate evidence. Accepted blockchain timestamp reports as proof of a creation date in a copyright case — calling them "legitimate and compelling."

Important nuance: French law classified this as "imperfect proof" (preuve imparfaite) — subject to the judge's assessment alongside other evidence, not an absolute "authentic deed." The decisive combination: blockchain timestamp + bailiff's verification report.

2024

U.S. District Court, D.C.

Applied the Daubert admissibility standard — accepted blockchain records as reliable evidence, characterizing the blockchain as "a decentralized, immutable, chronological, public ledger." Blockchain records now meet the federal reliability threshold.

Daubert is the gold standard for scientific/technical evidence reliability in U.S. federal courts. Passing it means the methodology itself is sound.

Jul 2025

9th Circuit, U.S.

The highest U.S. appellate court to address blockchain evidence accepted blockchain-recorded creation metadata as factual evidence — finding that records "become permanently stored on the blockchain." Directionally supportive of timestamping as evidence.

Case involved NFT transaction records (Yuga Labs v. Ripps) — not document timestamping specifically, but the court's reasoning about blockchain immutability applies broadly.

2016–now

U.S. Legislation

Vermont, Arizona, Illinois, and Delaware have enacted blockchain-specific evidence legislation. Federal Rules of Evidence 902(13) & (14), amended 2017, allow self-authentication of electronic records — structurally aligned with blockchain's cryptographic properties.

What "imperfect proof" means strategically: Blockchain evidence isn't a magic bullet — it's subject to the judge's assessment alongside other materials. But in a dispute, that's exactly where you want to be. A blockchain timestamp isn't someone's word, a screenshot, or a server log someone controls. It's the most tamper-resistant piece of evidence in the room. The Marseille ruling showed that the winning combination is: TrustAnchor's immutable timestamp + Sabo PR's documented chain of custody. That's a partnership model, not just a tool recommendation.
The proactive advantage: Courts have specifically noted that proactive timestamping — anchoring a document before a dispute arises — carries significantly more weight than timestamping after the fact. The entire value of TrustAnchor is that it happens before anyone has a reason to create a competing record. That window closes the moment a crisis begins.

Clarity

What TrustAnchor is.
And what it isn't.

It is

A timestamped, cryptographically sealed record of content at the moment of registration — independent of any company, platform, or institution.
A notary whose seal can never be revoked, forged, or subpoenaed away — because it's not held by any single party.
A pre-crisis tool. The guardrail, not the ambulance.
The most tamper-resistant piece of a broader evidentiary picture — and the only piece that has to exist before the crisis begins.
Already tested under Daubert reliability standards in U.S. federal court. Accepted as evidence in France, with U.S. appellate precedent supporting blockchain immutability.

It isn't

A legal service. It doesn't provide legal advice or guarantee outcomes.
Proof that the content is true — only that it existed, unaltered, at that moment. Provenance, not truth.
Perfect proof on its own. French courts classify it as "imperfect proof" subject to judicial assessment alongside other evidence — which is exactly how any good evidence strategy works.
Automatically admissible without preparation. Like any evidence, it requires authentication and documented chain of custody. The difference: it's built to survive that process.
A replacement for legal counsel or PR strategy — it's the record they'll need when the strategy requires one.
